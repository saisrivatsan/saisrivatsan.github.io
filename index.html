I am a fifth year PhD candidate in Computer Science at Harvard University, where I am fortunate to be advised by Prof. David Parkes. My research explores topics at the intersection of Deep Learning, Algorithmic Economics and Multi-Agent Systems.

During my PhD, I spent two years as a Student Researcher at Google Research. I collaborated with the Algorithms and Optimization Team in NYC on topics at the intersection of mechanism design and LLMs, and with the Market Algorithms team in Mountain View on Auctions and Reinforcement Learning.

Before coming to Harvard, I was a Research Fellow at Microsoft Research, India with the Machine Learning and Optimization group. I received a B.Tech in Electrical Engineering with a minor in Computer Science from the Indian Institute of Technology, Kharagpur.

Research Interests

  • Machine Learning for Mechanism Design
    My research focuses on how learning-based methods can be applied to problems in algorithmic design, particularly in settings involving strategic agents. I’ve worked on a range of problems—from auctions and matching markets to data markets—developing new frameworks that help uncover new mechanisms for allocation and pricing or protocols for matching.

  • AI for Science
    A guiding influence in my work is the broader vision of AI for Science. Although my work has primarily focused on economic systems, it shares a common structure with efforts in areas like drug discovery and materials science, where researchers are building new architectures and modeling approaches that encode domain intuition. I’m excited to help develop toolboxes that can help accelerate scientific discovery as well.

  • LLMs and Multi-Agent Systems
    Recently, I’ve been exploring projects at the intersection of LLMs and multi-agent systems, with a focus on what market design looks like when LLMs act as economic agents. This opens up new questions around robustness, equilibrium behavior, and design principles for markets populated by agentic models.
